Sushi

Food businesses containing the word ‘sushi’ are almost exclusively a phenomenon of the inner-city and inner western corridor stretching through Chelsea, Kensington and Hammersmith.

Local Moran’s I values for spatial autocorrelation show clustering of wards in the inner-city and wards immediately south of the city where there are similarly high-density values.

The location quotient which moderates the effect of the inner-city, normalizing by the total number of food businesses, shows that there is still a much greater concentration of sushi stores in the inner-city and inner western corridor relative to the rest of London.

There is a moderately strong positive correlation between the relative concentration (location quotient) of food business names containing the word ‘sushi’ and median ward income. In conclusion this analysis shows that sushi is most likely to be on the menu for lunch in affluent inner-city areas.
